Introduction
 LAC region
Latin America and the Caribbean (hereafter “LAC”) is a heterogenous region that goes from the Rio Grande in the north, bordering the United States, to Antarctica in the south. In the west it goes from Easter Island in the Pacific to Dominican Republic in the Caribbean, in the east. It’s an immense land expanse of over 20 million square kilometres covering two hemispheres, three oceans (Pacific, Atlantic, Antarctic) and a multitude of ecosystems. 
The LAC region is home to the world’s largest river, the Amazon, and the driest desert, the Atacama, with climatic conditions ranging from tropical (covering most of the region), to temperate, to arid. Long coastlines, tropical forests and mountain ranges characterise the region’s physical geography, this facilitates a range of biomes making the region the home of largest biodiversity in the world. 
The region comprises three sub-regions including South America, spanning 18 million square km, Central and North America, spanning 2.5 million square km, and the Caribbean, spanning 230 thousand square km. Together these regions represent just under 4% of the global landmass.
The region is composed of 48 separate administrative units including 33 independent countries and 15 dependent territories, that have a range of governance arrangements principally presidential systems, although there are some parliamentarian and dependent territories. 
 Population
The LAC region has a total of 662 million inhabitants, representing approximately 8.2% of the global population of principally European, African, Asian, and Indigenous descent. However, the region is much more diverse in its multicultural heritage and ethnic composition, although most people speak Spanish, French, and English, over 500 languages are spoken, most of which are cross-border languages (reference).
The region’s indigenous populations, while of great variety, contributes to a shared identity and understandings, especially with respect to nature and biodiversity. The Caribbean, in particular, boasts of a diverse yet distinctive cultural expression. This is due in part to the shared history of colonisation, slavery and forced displacement of African, Indian, and Chinese populations, and resulting social resistance, giving rise to a strong sense of cultural identity. Along with the six official languages, the sub-region is home to a rich host of Creole languages.
The region has undergone a swift demographic transition from high levels of mortality and fertility in the 1950s to low levels for both in recent decades, resulting in an aging population which has generated a number of economic and social challenges.
 Urbanisation
The population is predominantly urban reaching 81.2% in 2020, although there are some differences across all three sub-regions, where South America has the highest proportion with 85% of the population living in urban areas, followed by Central and North America, with 75%, and the Caribbean, with 67% of the population. Countries such as Chile, Argentina, Venezuela, Uruguay, Brazil, Mexico, and Peru have the highest urban population in the region and even the world.
The Caribbean is the sub-region with the highest population density having 1.87 persons per hectare in 2021, followed by Central and North America with 0.73 persons per hectare, and South America with 0.24 persons per hectare. 
 Natural Resources and the Environment
The LAC region comprises several types of climates, including tropical rainforest and tropical savanna, humid subtropical, mild Mediterranean, marine west coast, steppe, desert and highlands. It boasts of many types of natural vegetation, including tropical forest, deciduous and coniferous forest, tropical and temperate grassland, chaparral, desert scrub and highlands. The enormous climatic variability contained in the region is in large part due to the large difference in elevation, from lowlands running from Mexico to Brazil, to Andean peaks just under 7,000 metres, the region’s sheer size, covering […] square kilometres, and the impact of warm currents from the Atlantic Ocean, and cold currents from the Pacific Ocean.
Volcanic mountain ranges are present in Guatemala, Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and Panama. Volcanic eruptions and earthquakes occur frequently in this region. Volcanic activity can destroy towns and cities. It also contributes to the rich, fertile soils of the region.
The region’s diverse and varied ecosystems provide a number of natural resources that are the basis of the economy. For example:
  • Forestry is a major economic activity of the Amazon River basin. Many high-value tree species, such as mahogany and rosewood, are native to the rainforest. Chile is an important exporter of wood chips, plywood and paper pulp.
  • Marine fisheries are the most important economic activity along LAC’s Pacific coast, although overfishing has depleted many fish populations. The Humboldt current along the south Pacific brings nutrient-rich waters to the coast, creating a rich and diverse fishery industry in Chile and Peru. Chile is a global exporter of farm-raised salmon and trout, and Ecuador is an important shrimp exporter.
  • Fertile soils and mild climates have supported an agricultural economy in many. Brazil is the world’s largest exporter of coffee. Brazil, Venezuela and Ecuador compete with tropical African countries for cacao. Similarly, Brazil and Argentina have supported industrial crops, such as corn and soybeans,
  • The Pampa’s vast pastures feed South America’s livestock. Brazil is the world’s third largest beef exporter; Argentina is also an important beef exporter. Grazing animals such a sheep, llamas, alpacas and vicuñas are bred for their meat and wool, used in high quality textiles exported worldwide.
  • The mining industry is one of LAC’s most important sectors, as the region is heavily endowed in valuable minerals. The South American continent contains about one fifth of the world-s iron ore reserves, more than one quarter of the world’s copper reserves (mostly in Peru and Chile), as well as tin, lead and zinc (found mostly in Bolivia, Brazil, Peru and Argentina).
  • Oil and natural gas deposits. Oil and gas extraction is the dominant industry of Venezuela, accounting for about one third of its GDP.
  • Coral reefs are very present in the Caribbean, and surround islands such as the Bahamas, Antigua and Barbados.
 Economy
Although the economy in the region is diverse, most activities are focussed on the use and processing of natural resources and the natural environment. In 2021 total GDP was over 5 trillion USD (current prices) representing around 5% of the global economy (ECLAC).
Between 1990 and 2019, the region’s GDP growth has oscillated around 2.7%, reaching a peak of 6.1% in 2006, and several contractionary troughs in 1995 and 2016. The region’s recovery from the COVID-19 crisis has seen a growth of 7% in 2021.
Excluding Argentina, Haiti, Suriname and Venezuela, the Consumer Price Index stabilised around 6.7% (yearly, December to December) from 1997 to 2020, following intense volatility in consumer prices, which saw peaks of 917.5% in 1993 and 1451.4% in 1990. In 2021, CPI rose by 12.5% as compared to the previous year.
Tourism contributes enormously to the region’s GDP. With the highest countries located mostly in the Caribbean, including Aruba, Antigua and Barbuda, Turks and Caicos, Montserrat, Grenada, Saint Lucia, Sint Maarten and Saint Kitts and Nevis. On average, inbound tourism expenditure contributes to 18.2% of GDP (2018) (UNWTO / World Tourism Organization / Compendium of Tourism Statistics).
Public debt is high, reaching an average of 65% of GDP in 2020. There is, however, a large divergence between sub-regions, as the countries with the highest public debt in 2020 were Barbados (142%), Belize, Bahamas, Suriname, Dominica, Jamaica, Bahamas and Antigua and Barbuda. South American countries with high public debt include Argentina (102.8%), Brazil (88.8%), Colombia (71.5%) and Uruguay (65.4%) (ECLAC) 
The balance of payments in the LAC region has mostly been negative over the past three decades, with the exception of the period 2003-2007, where the balance of payments reached the peak of 50 billion dollars in 2006. In 2021, the balance of payments was in a deficit of 75.5 billion dollars, indicating a larger value of imports than exports (ECLAC).
The 10 leading products exported by the region include (in order, 2021): crude petroleum, copper ore, iron ore, soya beans, motor vehicles for the transport of persons and goods (respectively), gold, automatic data processing machines, refined copper, and petroleum oils (COMTRADE, ECLAC).
Economic activities range from mineral and petroleum extraction and processing in South America and Mexico, to industrial agriculture and intensive fishing. Mineral production includes a quarter of the world’s copper, a fifth of the iron ore reserves, tin, lead, and zinc. Also, there are major oil and natural gas reserves in South America, although energy production in many countries is based on hydroelectricity with, in some cases, massive infrastructure projects, for example in Brazil, Colombia and Peru. All these activities generate significant environmental impacts.
Light industry and manufacturing is also relevant in the larger countries such as Brazil and Mexico, the former with a significant maquila industry associated with the North American Free Trade Agreement. 
Beautiful landscapes and beaches with a mild sunny climate are the basis of a very important tourism industry, particularly in the Central America and the Caribbean.
Finally, related to these activities, an increasingly important service and financial industry has developed, particularly in the large metropolis, such as Buenos Aires, Sao Paulo, Buenos Aires and Santiago.
Notwithstanding these activities agriculture remains the principal is not the key economic, and above all, social activity. The region’s cash crops include coffee and cacao, as well as industrial crops such as corn, soya, and livestock. But it is through agriculture’s role as a subsistence crop that it remains a relevant with over 15 million smallholder farms in LAC and 20‑30% of the workforce in the Caribbean associated with these activities. 
These economic activities have considerable environmental impacts but, what may be even more significant, global, and local environmental change will generate considerable risks to the region’s future economic development. Therefore, the regions social and economic wellbeing is inextricably connected to the sustainability of the natural environment and the quality of the ecosystem services provided.
 Social Conditions
The LAC region is characterised by high levels of income inequality, spatial segregation and unequal access to health and urban infrastructure. Income inequality quantified by the Gini coefficientis similar for  both urban and rural areas at around 0.4 in 2021. However, there are considerable difference across countries, the Gini coefficient ranges from 0.395 in the Dominican Republic and 0.402 in Uruguay, to 0.528 in Colombia and 0.537 in Brazil, (2021) (ECLAC, BADEHOG).
Poverty is a major issue and there is a considerable difference between rural and urban populations. The percentage of the population living in extreme poverty, in urban areas is 32.3% and in rural areas is 12.9% generating a number of challenges for the provision of social services and infrastructure. While this gap has narrowed since the beginning of the millennium, further progress in the past decade has stagnated and the economic crisis following the pandemic has aggravated the situation.
Of particular concern in the region concern is the especially vulnerable situation faced by women and children, particularly in the labour force. The average participation rate of the economically active population was 63.2% in 2021, being 76% for men and 51.5% for women. The differences in opportunities and outcomes between rural and urban populations, as well as that of indigenous communities are also considerable (ECLAC, BADEHOG)..
Poverty levels varie considerably across the region Across all countries, women and children have higher poverty rates. Additionally, the largest gaps are found in countries with low poverty rates, such as Brazil, Chile, the Dominican Republic, and Uruguay. Informality remains a key challenge. In LAC, both informal and mixed households account for two‑thirds of the total population.
These social conditions suggest that large groups of the population may be very vulnerable to global and local environmental change, given the close relationship between economic subsistence and environmental conditions. 
 The Pandemic and Recent Economic Conditions
The region has been especially affected by the COVID-19 pandemic, experiencing one of the highest mortality rates in the world, Brazil, Peru and Ecuador were especially affected with high mortality rates and considerable economic and social impacts. This has shown vulnerabilities and difficulties for managing the health and economic crises stemming from the pandemic, as the region experienced the biggest decline in life expectancy at birth (Latin America and the Caribbean lost 2.9 years of life expectancy at birth between 2019 and 2021).
The economic response from LAC countries has also been much weaker than in developed economies. The set of measures announced by the advanced economies amounted to an estimated USD 14.9 trillion by the end of September 2021, while the emerging countries had mobilized some USD 2.7 trillion (IMF, 2021). Moreover, the available fiscal space to underpin the recovery through policy support has narrowed for emerging countries.
The LAC region experienced a robust economic rebound in 2021, but growth is expected to slow in 2022, with a possible recession in 2023, driven by an increasingly adverse global backdrop, fiscal and monetary stimuli rollbacks, and low potential growth (https://repositorio.cepal.org/bitstream/handle/11362/48415/1/S2201062_en.pdf) . Potential gross domestic product (GDP) per‑capita growth in LAC remains stable but at low levels (OECD et al., 2022). Over the last decades, the per‑capita GDP gap between LAC and advanced economies has narrowed, but gains stopped after 2015 as economic growth stalled. 
Moreover, post-pandemic recovery has been uneven. While several countries regained pre‑pandemic GDP levels, decreasing tourism flows and limited fiscal space for stimulus constrained a growth in the Caribbean and Mexico. The reduced trade exposure to Russia and Ukraine, which does not exceed 1% of the region’s total trade, has limited the direct impact of the war on the LAC region. However, the spike in commodity prices has affected the region’s economies in different ways, with net exporters in South America benefiting while Central American and Caribbean countries have experienced the opposite (OECD et al., 2022).
The COVID‑19 pandemic aggravated social challenges in the region, leading to increased levels of poverty and inequality. Estimates suggest that, by the end of 2022, 33.7% of the population will be in poverty and 14.9% in extreme poverty, the highest level since 2008, before the global financial crisis.
 The Economy and the Environment
LAC’s economic development is inexorably related to the natural environment. The economy both depends on and impacts the natural environment. 
High urbanisation rates with inadequate economic and social infrastructure has generated considerable local pollution. Although motorisation rates remain low at an average of 200 vehicles per thousand inhabitants (2015), it varies considerably across countries the highest rates being Puerto Rico (904), Barbados (387), Suriname (349), Bahamas (336), Argentina (317) and Mexico (300). This compares to [OECD average]. 126 million registered vehicles in 2015, including passenger transport vehicles and commercial vehicles, with the countries with the highest numbers being Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Colombia, Venezuela, Puerto Rico and Peru (OICA / International Organization of Motor Vehicle Manufacturers / International Organization of Motor Vehicle Manufacturers (OICA), Number of vehicles in use 2015 – worldwide).
Emissions from private passenger cars is one of the principal drivers of local air pollution and GHG emissions form the region.

For example, while fish capture production has seen a downward trend since the mid-1990s, totalling around 12.7 million tonnes in 2020, aquaculture production has boomed over the same period, reaching close to 3.8 million tonnes in 2020, indicating a turn from fish capture to farming.
