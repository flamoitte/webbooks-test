 Context
 Issues at stake 
Greenhouse gas (GHGs) emissions from human activities disturb the radiative energy balance of the earth-atmosphere system. The concentration of GHG emissions in the atmosphere exacerbates the natural greenhouse effect, leading to temperature changes and other disruptions of the earth's climate which can be further aggravated by the existence of “tipping points” that can accelerate temperature change with non-linear effects.
Land use changes and forestry also play a role by altering the amount of greenhouse gases released or captured by carbon sinks. National emissions are also affected by changes in global demand and supply patterns with increasing trade flows and the displacement of carbon-intensive production abroad. Reductions in domestic emissions can thus be partially or wholly offset elsewhere in the world.
Climate change is of global concern due to its considerable environmental impacts and the consequences on social and economic systems. It threatens ecosystems and biodiversity, affects water resources, human settlements and the frequency and scale of extreme weather events, with significant impacts on food security, human well-being, socio-economic activities, and economic output.
 Policy challenges
The main challenge is to stabilise GHG concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would limit dangerous interference with the climate system, and to adapt to and manage risks from climate change.
  • This implies implementing national and international low-carbon strategies and further decoupling GHG emissions from economic growth. It also requires increasing the share of renewable energy sources in the supply mix and reducing energy intensity by adopting energy-efficient production processes, as well as increasing the energy efficiency of consumer goods and services.
  • With increasing trade flows, interdependent global value chains and the relocation of carbon-intensive production abroad, reductions in domestic emissions can be partially or wholly offset elsewhere in the world. Domestic mitigation efforts must therefore be placed in a global context and must build on an understanding of carbon flows associated with international trade and final domestic demand.
  • Ensuring the correct pricing of environmental externalities through a mix of market-based instruments plays an important role in this transition. Key policies include adopting effective carbon pricing and environmentally-related taxation, as well as removing harmful government subsidies and support for fossil fuels. 
  • Beyond these steps, governments must align policies across a diverse range of non-climate areas including transport, housing, construction, spatial planning, agriculture, and development cooperation. And they must consider synergies between emissions’ reduction, adaptation strategies and broader well-being objectives such as reduced air pollution and improved health.
The Paris Agreement (2015) that strengthens the global response to the threat of climate change. The objective is to keep the average global temperature rise this century well below 2 degrees Celsius and as close as possible to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. Parties have expressed their commitments to 2025 or 2030 through nationally determined contributions (NDCs), including a regular report on their emissions and implementation efforts.

33 LAC countries have submitted commitments to the UNFCCC, with the exception of Puerto Rico. By 2022, X Latin American countries updated their NDC targets, (Mexico, Guatemala, El Salvador, Haiti, Bahamas, Brazil, Bolivia, Dominica, and Uruguay in 2022), many of which presented an increase in ambition Moreover, Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica and Peru have committed net zero targets. Chile, Colombia and Costa Rica detail separate reduction and removal targets in their commitments, while no net-zero target comprises emissions from international aviation and shipping sectors. 
 Indicators
The indicators presented here relate to both climate mitigation and climate adaptation. They show greenhouse gas and carbon emissions, developments in energy supply and energy intensity, and the share of renewable energy sources in the supply mix, as well as exposure to major climate related hazards.

If LULUCF is considered, the increase rate rises to 54.4% (OECD et al., 2022). 
In 2019, the LAC’s average per capita GHG emissions level equalled the global average and was lower than the OECD average. However, this varies considerably across countries and sub regions. The Caribbean countries show high intensities due to emissions generated by non-residents (i.e. tourism), although Suriname, Chile, Argentina, Guyana and Venezuela also have high intensity levels (OECD et al., 2022). 
The structure of emissions in LAC differs from that of the EU and OECD countries. Whereas the energy sector (which includes building, electricity and heat, transport, fugitive emissions, manufacturing and construction, and other fuel combustion) accounts for more than 80% in OECD and EU countries’ total emissions, it only represents 43.5% in LAC countries. Agriculture is the second source of emissions accounting for 25.3% of the total, more than double the OECD. Land use change and forestry (LULUCF) which act as a net sink in the OECD, represent 19.5% of LAC emissions (OECD et al., 2022). 
South America’s high emitting sectors are agriculture (28.5%), LULUCF (23.8%), and transport (13.4%). The Caribbean differs slightly, with electricity and heat (24.8%) followed by agriculture (15.6%) and LULUCF (13.4%, very similar to transport at 11.1%). In Central America the electricity and heat sector accounts for 23.8% of emissions, while transport represents 21.4%, followed by agriculture with 16% (OECD et al., 2022). 
Since 1990, emissions from almost all sectors have grown continuously, with the greatest increase in the energy sector. Emissions from agriculture increased by about 32% due to the rise in extensive grazing systems in South and Central America until 2010. While emissions from the industrial and waste sectors are lower, their growth rates have been significant (193% and 108%, respectively since 1990) (OECD et al., 2022). 
The growth of emissions from the transport sector contributes the most to the increase of energy‑related GHG emissions in LAC, followed by electricity and heat production. Forest loss is a major concern in the region, explained by new land uses for agriculture, forestry, and stockbreeding, and, to a lesser extent, by the expansion of cities and highway building. Deforestation by logging in Brazil has accelerated since 2012, and particularly in recent years. Although the forest areas involved are smaller, Nicaragua and Paraguay also have had high loss rates in the last 20 years. On the other hand, Costa Rica and Chile managed to increase forest cover (OECD et al., 2022). 
Transport is one of the main sources of air pollution and high GHG emission levels, mainly due to the large size of many LAC countries. In Latin American cities, private motorised transport is responsible for 75% of CO2 emissions and produces 82% of PM10 pollutants. Moreover, the Caribbean, land‑based transportation accounts for the second‑largest share of fossil‑energy consumption. The subregion has one of the highest vehicle motorisation rates in the world, with 201 vehicles per 1 000 inhabitants. In Latin American and Caribbean countries, the urban layout and the availability and quality of public transport influence automobile use and shape car-dependent and sprawled territories. The GHG emissions of the region’s transport sector relative to GDP are 2.2 times as high as in Europe and 1.3 times as high as in Asia, which means there is ample scope to increase carbon efficiency (OECD et al., 2022).

 Drivers
Recent trends and developments
The region’s primary energy supply mix remains dominated by fossil fuels (66% in 2020), although renewables account for a much larger share (33%) than in OECD (12%) and the rest of the world (13%). The primary source of renewables in LAC is hydroelectric power (9%), biofuels such as firewood and bagasse (18.8%), solar and wind (5.1%) and geothermal (0.9%) (OECD et al., 2022). 
LAC countries have been implementing policies designed to reduce their dependence on fossil fuels. However, emissions generated by oil and gas represent the primary source of pollution across the region, reaching 90% or above of CO2e in countries such as Costa Rica, El Salvador, Paraguay, and Uruguay. Although energy produced with coal has been decreasing across the region, some LAC countries remain highly dependent (e.g. Chile, Colombia, the Dominican Republic and Guatemala). Nevertheless, countries are increasingly implementing policies to reduce fossil fuel dependence, for example, in 2019, Chile approved a Coal Phase‑Out Plan, aiming to close all coal‑fired power plants by 2040,5 of 28 existing plants have already been closed. The Caribbean is highly dependent on imported fossil energy. Only Trinidad and Tobago, Suriname and recently Guyana have significant domestic energy resources (OECD et al., 2022). 
The region has slightly decreased its energy intensity in the last three decades, but mainly because of limited industrial development, the impacts generated by COVID‑19 and Russia’s invasion of Ukraine (OECD et al., 2022). 
Over the last two decades, many LAC countries have made substantial progress in building renewable energy markets and diversifying their energy mix. In 2020, renewable energy accounted for 61% of regional electricity generation, broken down as 75% from hydroelectricity and 25% from solar, wind, biomass and geothermal. Central America has shown the greatest increases in renewables in the last two decades, from 65% to 77%, followed by the Caribbean with a modest increase of 3 percentage points. However, significant variations exist across the region,. While Brazil generates 84% of its electric power from renewables, Jamaica relies on imported oil derivatives for 87% of its electricity generation (OECD et al., 2022).
LAC countries producing electricity from renewables, given their abundance of low‑cost renewable energy and relatively clean electricity matrices, can position themselves as green hydrogen industrial hubs. This could help support decarbonisation of hard‑to‑abate sectors such as heavy industries and transportation – for which no viable alternatives to fossil fuels currently exist.
Nevertheless, since renewable energy is focussed on hydroelectricity, representing X% of total energy supply, this also implies a significant risk, changing rainfall patterns increasing draught may affect the capacity of energy systems. Chile is a case in point the extended drought has affected considerably hydroelectric generation capacity.
 
Box 2. Renewables in LAC: The RELAC initiative
Renewables in Latin America and the Caribbean (RELAC) is a regional initiative created in 2019 within the framework of the United Nations Climate Action Summit. It has 15 member countries in the LAC region that have voluntarily agreed to promote renewables with: 1) a concrete goal; 2) a monitoring scheme; and 3) an operating structure designed to support countries in the process. The initiative’s general goals are to accelerate the carbon neutrality of electricity systems in LAC; improve the resilience, competitiveness and sustainability of the sector; and create green jobs, improving air quality and minimising harmful health effects for Latin Americans. To do this, RELAC aims to establish a climate action platform for LAC countries and international organisations to enable sharing sustainable solutions.
The RELAC initiative aims to reach at least 70% of renewable energy participation in the region’s electricity matrix by 2030. Two conditions are needed to achieve these targets. The first is to invest 1.3% of regional gross domestic product (GDP) for ten years, equivalent to USD 114 per capita (i.e. USD 80 billion in constant 2010 prices), with some countries needing a greater or lesser proportion of GDP depending on local circumstances. The second is to increase renewables technologies (mostly solar and wind) in line with the targets of Sustainable Development Goal 7 (SDG 7) on access to affordable and clean energy. These actions could create 7 million green jobs and reduce GHG emissions by 30% by 2030.
Source: https://hubenergia.org/en/relac 
 Comparability and interpretation
The CO2 emission estimates are affected by the quality of the underlying energy data, but in general the comparability across countries is quite good. Carbon productivity indicators inform on the relative decoupling between economic activity and GHG emissions. They provide insight into how much carbon productivity has improved. They also measure how much of the improvement is due to domestic policies and how much to displacement or substitution effects. Reductions in national emissions can also be achieved by offshoring domestic production and, thus, the related emissions. Evidence of decoupling based on domestic emissions, therefore, may reveal only part of the story.
Data quality is not homogeneous across countries. In some countries, data is based on secondary sources, and where incomplete, estimates were made by the IEA. In general, data is likely to be more accurate for production and trade than for international bunkers or stock changes; and statistics for biofuels and waste are less accurate than those for traditional commercial energy data. The supply structure, which may vary considerably among countries is dependent on final demands by industry, transport and the household sector, and is highly influenced by national energy policies and endowments in energy resources.
