Climate Change mitigation
 Key messages
 
  • LAC's share in global GHG emissions is proportional to its share in the total world population (8.4%), slightly higher than its share of total gross domestic product (GDP) (6.4%), but lower than the per capita emissions in other regions with similar development levels. Its energy mix is also greener: renewable energy resources represent 33% of its total energy supply compared to 13% globally. 
  • Greenhouse gas emissions from the LAC region represented around 6.7% of global emissions in 2019 (excluding land use change and forestry).
  • The per capita level of GHG emissions in LAC has reached similar levels to the global average, while remaining significantly lower than the OECD average. 
  • Since 1990, emissions from almost all sectors have grown continuously, in particular in the energy sector, driven by the growth of emissions from the transport, followed by electricity and heat production. Emissions from agriculture increased by about 32% due to the rise in livestock breeding in South and Central America until 2010.
  • In line with GDP growth, the LAC region has experienced a steady increase in GHG emissions until 2014. Emissions stabilised and even decreased afterwards despite a rebound in economic activity, suggesting a relative decoupling.
  • 15 countries have declared net zero targets with varying scopes of gases considered, 3 have achieved net zero, and 15 do not yet have a target.
  • 25 countries have submitted emission reduction targets for 2030 as part of their NDCs. Of the 8 countries that do not have emission reduction targets as part of their NDC, 3 are already carbon neutral. 
  • Mean population exposure to PM2.5 across all countries of the LAC region is significantly above the guideline set by the WHO of annual mean concentrations of PM2.5, the highest being Peru, Suriname, Trinidad and Tobago, Belize, Grenada and Chile.
  • Fine particulate emissions are responsible for a large number of premature deaths in LAC countries. Linked to respiratory infections, ischemic heart disease and stroke, cancer and chronic respiratory diseases, an average of 260 premature deaths per million inhabitants is attributed to PM2.5.
 Context
 The issue
Emissions of greenhouse gases (GHGs) from human activities disturb the radiative energy balance of the earth-atmosphere system. They exacerbate the natural greenhouse effect, leading to temperature changes and other disruption of the earth's climate. Land use changes and forestry also play a role by altering the amount of greenhouse gases captured or released by carbon sinks. Carbon dioxide (CO2) from the combustion of fossil fuels and deforestation is a major contributor to greenhouse gases and a key factor in countries’ ability to mitigate climate change.
GHG are often released from the same sources as air pollutants. Hence mitigating GHG emissions also reduces air pollutants, such as fine particulate matter (PM2.5) and improves air quality with benefits for human health, especially in densely populated areas. Air pollution abatement measures in turn reduce GHG emissions. Climate change is of global concern due to the environmental impacts and its consequences on social and economic systems. It threatens ecosystems and biodiversity, affects water resources, human settlements and the frequency and scale of extreme weather events, with significant impacts on food security, human well-being, socio-economic activities and economic output. 
 Policy challenges
The main challenges are to mitigate GHG emissions and stabilise GHG concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would limit dangerous interference with the climate system.
This implies implementing low-carbon strategies and further decoupling GHG emissions from economic growth, increasing the share of renewable energy sources in the supply mix, reducing energy intensity by adopting energy-efficient production processes and increasing the energy efficiency of consumer goods and services. It also implies strengthening the capacity of the environment to store carbon by protecting ecosystems or increasing their capacity to capture carbon emissions, whis is an important challenge in the LAC region. Domestic mitigation efforts must also be placed in a global context and must build on a good understanding of carbon flows associated with international trade and final domestic demand. 
Another important challenge is to develop and implement strategies that reduce emissions of multiple air pollutants and greenhouse gases at least cost and that offer “win-win” solutions for both health and climate. This implies implementing effective pollution prevention and control policies and sustainable transport and mobility policies, beyond sustainable energy policies. 
Air emissions can be reduced by substituting dirty fuels for cleaner ones, focusing development on cleaner industries, reducing consumption of polluting products and adopting cleaner technologies. Behavioural and lifestyle changes are also important. Both the sources of air pollution and severity of exposure vary across and within countries. Hence it is important to tailor policies to specific local circumstances. For example, more stringent measures are required in densely populated areas or for emission sources located upwind from urban areas.
 Measuring progress and performance 
Environmental performance can be assessed against domestic objectives and international goals and commitments to reduce GHG and air pollutant emissions. This is can be referred to as “distance-to-target”.
The centrepiece of the international climate governance regime is the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) negotiated at the United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED) in Rio de Janeiro in 1992. The Convention established a broad set of substantive principles, including the “common but differentiated responsibilities”, but does not contain binding commitments to reduce GHG emissions. [add air pollution agreements].
At the 21st session of the Conference of Parties (COP) in Paris, on 12 December 2015, Parties to the UNFCCC reached a landmark agreement to combat climate change and to accelerate and intensify the actions and investments needed for a sustainable low-carbon future. The Paris Agreement (PA) sets out the goal of limiting average global warming to 2ºC above pre-industrial levels and identifies a further need for efforts to confine the increase to 1.5ºC. The basis of the PA is a non-binding “bottom-up” approach. Countries submit contributions reflecting their highest possible ambition; these are referred to as Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). NDCs present targets to progressively mitigate GHG emissions and enhance their adaptive capacity to climate change, together with information necessary for clarity, transparency and understanding.
Reducing negative impacts of degraded air quality is part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development (New York, September 2015) under Goal 3 “Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages” and under Goal 11 “Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable”.

 Climate objectives in the LAC region
Under the Paris Agreement, 33 LAC countries have submitted commitments to the UNFCCC, with the exception of Puerto Rico. However, there are differences across the countries’ NDCs in terms of clarity, information details, and gas and sectoral coverage. Only 20 countries presented NDCs that are clear enough to infer targets for 2030 and beyond.  
By 2022, 29 Latin American countries had updated their NDC targets, since their first submission1, many of which presented an increase in ambition. Moreover, 152 have committed to net zero targets by 2050 or before.3 However, countries have made commitments with different legal status. Chile is the only country whose targets are enshrined in a climate law, while three countries – Jamaica, Paraguay, and Trinidad and Tobago – have declared a net zero pledge, and eleven countries – Antigua and Barbuda, Argentina, Barbados, Belize, Brazil, Colombia, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Ecuador, Peru and Uruguay – have included the target in a policy document.
The scope of the net zero targets is of importance. Chile, Colombia and Costa Rica detail separate reduction and removal targets in their commitments, while no net-zero target comprises emissions from international aviation and shipping sectors. Importantly, only six countries cover all GHG in their pledges (Argentina, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ecuador and Peru), leaving a majority of countries with either unspecified pledges, or pledges limited to CO2. The latter is of particular concern in a region where emissions from methane-intensive sectors such as agriculture contribute to over a quarter of total emissions, missing the opportunity to meaningfully achieve net zero. Table 1 presents the type of targets and commitments presented by LAC countries. Countries have set out a range of possible policy approaches to comply with their commitments. These vary from direct regulations, carbon pricing, reducing deforestation, among other approaches and efforts.

 Greenhouse gas emissions
 Recent trends and developments 
GHG emissions data for the LAC region is limited, with no comprehensive up to date official data and considerable gaps for most countries. Estimates suggest total gross emissions (excluding Land Use Change and Forestry, or LUCF) amount to about 3.2 Gt of CO2e in 2019, representing approximately around 6.7% of global GHG emissionsFigure 4. Despite not being a major contributor to climate change, emissions rose by 61% between 1990 and 2019 and the LAC region has a significant potential for natural carbon capture, particularly around the amazon river basin. The region can therefore play a crucial role in future global mitigation efforts.
Emissions grew steadily in line with GDP growth until 2014, then stabilised and even decreased despite a rebound in economic activity, suggesting a relative decoupling. However, there are differences between sub-regions, reflecting their size and the level of economic activity. South America generates most of LAC GHG emissions (71%), followed by Central America (24%) and the Caribbeans (4%). The overall trend is thus driven by South America, where emissions increased by 61% between 1990 and 2019, followed by Central America (+68%) and the Caribbean (+26%) (Figure 5).4
GHG emissions also vary considerably across countries. Brazil contributed about 32% of LAC’s gross total GHG emissions in 2019 while the top five emitters (Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Venezuela, and Colombia) contributed about 78% and the top ten emitters contributed 90%.5 The remaining 23 LAC countries generated 10% of the region’s gross total GHG emissions. 
The structure of emissions in LAC differs from that of the EU and OECD countries. In LAC, agriculture is the main emitter (25.3% of the region’s net emissions). In the OECD area, the production of electricity and heat generates most emissions (about a third of net GHG emissions), followed by transport (about 25.5%). Land use, land use change and forestry (LULUCF) act as a net sink in the OECD, whereas they generate about 19.5% of net emissions in LAC (Figure 7). 
These trends are driven by the emission structure of South America where agriculture and LULUCF account for a third and a fourth of the sub-region’s net emissions, respectively. This in turn, reflects the significance of agriculture in the economy as well as the massive deforestation occurring in the region. Forest loss is a major concern in the region, mainly driven by extensive conversion of land for agriculture, forestry, and, to a lesser extent, the expansion of urban areas. Deforestation in Brazil has accelerated since 2012 and although the forest areas involved are smaller, Nicaragua and Paraguay also have had high loss rates in the last 20 years. On the other hand, Costa Rica and Chile managed to increase their forest cover through successful afforestation policies (OECD et al., 2022[15]). 
Central America has a profile more similar to OECD countries with energy generation being the first source of emissions, followed by transport. In the Caribbean, energy generation is also the main source of GHGs but due to the relatively small size of Caribbean islands, transport is only the fourth source of emissions after agriculture and LULUCF (Figure 7 and Figure 8).
Since 1990, emissions from almost all sectors have grown continuously, in particular in the energy sector. This trend was driven by the growth of emissions from the transport, followed by electricity and heat production. Emissions from agriculture increased by about 32% due to the rise in livestock breeding in South and Central America until 2010. While emissions from industrial processes and waste are lower, they have significantly increased (193% and 108%, respectively since 1990) (OECD et al., 2022[15]). 
Transport is a major source of GHG emission, mainly due to the large size of many LAC countries and reliance of transport on petroleum. The Caribbean has one of the highest vehicle ownership rates in the world, with 201 vehicles per 1 000 inhabitants. In LAC countries, the urban layout and the availability and quality of public transport favour automobile use and shape car-dependent and sprawled territories. The GHG emission intensity of the transport sector (relative to GDP) is 2.2 times higher than in Europe and 1.3 times higher than Asia (OECD et al., 2022[15]).
In 2019, the per capita level of GHG emissions in LAC was similar to the global average but lower than the OECD one. However, there are significant differences across countries and sub regions. The Caribbean countries show high intensities due to emissions generated by non-residents (i.e., from tourism), although Suriname, Chile, Argentina, Guyana, and Venezuela also have high intensity levels (OECD et al., 2022[15]) (Figure 9 and Figure 10).
 Comparability, interpretation and data availability
GHG emissions
Official GHG emissions inventory data made available through the UNFCCC and OECD are characterised by missing observations for countries that have nonnegligible contribution to global emissions. For instance, between 1990 and 2017, official emissions data is missing for at least seven LAC countries in any given year. Because of the limitations missing observations put on analysis, estimated values from Climate Watch Historical GHG Emissions data provided by the World Resource Institute (WRI) is used (Climate Watch, 2022[16]). 
The Climate Watch data cover all parties to the UNFCCC, all gasses and main IPCC sectors. However, the dataset has limitations. It does not use countries’ inventory data and the data’s comparability to inventory data is not explicitly provided in the documentation. In addition, the data documentation states that it has substantial uncertainty. Further, aggregation of the main sector sources in the data does not always add up to the total reported in the data and, more importantly, the methodologies used for estimation of the observations is not explicitly stated in the documentation. The Climate Watch data documentation details the sources used, and the data remains the only comprehensive data that enables regional analysis. The Climate Watch data follows IPCC’s guidelines to compile the sectoral emissions drawing on sources that have been widely adopted including IEA’s GHG Emissions from Fossil Fuel Combustion, FAO’s agriculture and land use emissions data, US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)’s non-CO2 gases emissions data, and fossil related CO2 emissions from the Global Carbon Project. 
CO2 emissions from fuel combustion
The CO2 emission intensity estimates are affected by the quality of the underlying energy data, but in general the comparability across countries is quite good. Carbon productivity indicators inform on the relative decoupling between economic activity and GHG emissions. They provide insight into how much carbon productivity has improved. They also measure how much of the improvement is due to domestic policies and how much to displacement or substitution effects. Reductions in national emissions can also be achieved by offshoring domestic production and, thus, the related emissions. Evidence of decoupling based on domestic emissions, therefore, may reveal only part of the story.
 Energy mix
 Recent trends and developments
[start with a sentence on trends in energy supply and related intensities]
The supply mix remains dominated by fossil fuels (66% in 2020), although renewables account for a much larger share (33%) than in OECD (12%) and the rest of the world (13%). The main sources of renewables in LAC are hydroelectric power, and biofuels such as firewood and bagasse (OECD et al., 2022[15]). The use of renewables is very heterogenous in the region: in some countries such as Brazil, Costa Rica, Haiti, Honduras, Nicaragua, Paraguay and Uruguay, the share of renewables in the energy mix is above 40% while it is less than 10% in Argentina, Guyana, Mexico and Trinidad and Tobago. There is almost no use of nuclear energy in the region. Although energy produced with coal has been decreasing across the region, many LAC countries remain highly dependent (e.g., Chile, Colombia, the Dominican Republic and Guatemala) (OECD et al., 2022[15]). 
In 2020, renewable energy accounted for 61% of LAC electricity generation, broken down as 75% from hydroelectricity and 25% from solar, wind, biomass and geothermal. Central America has shown the greatest increases in renewables in the last two decades, from 65% to 77%, followed by the Caribbean with a modest increase of 3 percentage points. However, significant variations exist across the region. While Brazil generates 84% of its electric power from renewables, Jamaica relies on imported oil derivatives for 87% of its electricity generation (OECD et al., 2022[15]).
Since renewable energy is focused on hydroelectricity, representing 45% of total energy supply in Latin America, there is a significant risk that changing rainfall patterns and increasing drought may affect the capacity of energy systems to adapt. In Chile extended droughts have affected considerably hydroelectric generation capacity.
 
Box 1. Renewables in LAC: The RELAC initiative
Renewables in Latin America and the Caribbean (RELAC) is a regional initiative created in 2019 within the framework of the United Nations Climate Action Summit. It has 15 member countries in the LAC region that have voluntarily agreed to promote renewables with: 1) a concrete goal; 2) a monitoring scheme; and 3) an operating structure designed to support countries in the process. The initiative’s general goals are to accelerate the carbon neutrality of electricity systems in LAC; improve the resilience, competitiveness and sustainability of the sector; and create green jobs, improving air quality and minimising harmful health effects for Latin Americans. To do this, RELAC aims to establish a climate action platform for LAC countries and international organisations to enable sharing sustainable solutions.
The RELAC initiative aims to reach at least 70% of renewable energy participation in the region’s electricity matrix by 2030. Two conditions are needed to achieve these targets. The first is to invest 1.3% of regional gross domestic product (GDP) for ten years, equivalent to USD 114 per capita (i.e., USD 80 billion in constant 2010 prices), with some countries needing a greater or lesser proportion of GDP depending on local circumstances. The second is to increase renewables technologies (mostly solar and wind) in line with the targets of Sustainable Development Goal 7 (SDG 7) on access to affordable and clean energy. These actions could create 7 million green jobs and reduce GHG emissions by 30% by 2030.

 Comparability and interpretation
 Greenhouse gases and air pollution
Putting in place strategies that reduce both emissions of air pollutants and GHG is particularly important in LAC where air pollution is the leading environmental health risk and a major cause of environmental degradation (PAHO, 2023[30]). Pollutant emissions have also brought disturbances to the evaporation rate and atmospheric humidity in South America, thereby decreasing precipitation in the Amazon basin (Koren et al., 2004[32]). Some population groups are especially vulnerable to air pollution, the very young and the very old being the most at risk. However, information on the magnitude and incidence of specific health effects of air pollutants in the region is lacking.
Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) is a local air pollutant that seriously affects human health, predominantly caused by the increase in motorisation in the region, specifically diesel motors, the use of wood as fuel, and wildfires. Chronic exposure even to moderate levels of PM2.5 substantially increases the risk of heart disease and stroke and is an important cause of death in LAC countries (Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, 2019[33]). It also increases the risk of respiratory diseases, including lung cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and respiratory infections. Black carbon, a major component of PM2.5, is one of the largest contributors to global warming after CO2. It accelerates the melting of snow and ice and affects agricultural yields and food security. Black carbon has been associated with the accelerated loss of Andean glaciers whose size has decreased fourfold from 1964-1975 to 2010 (Rabatel et al., 2013[34]). , This affects the livelihoods of 85 million Andean inhabitants living across Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, Perú and Venezuela; 20 million depend on the glaciers for drinking water and agriculture. It also affects the generation of hydropower, a central source of energy in the region (World Bank & ICCI, 2013[35]).
Urban planning and policies to green the transport system have the power to significantly reduce PM2.5 world bank pollution levels. Understanding the relationship between pollution levels and characteristics of the urban environment is particularly useful for policymakers in the context of limited air quality data, as policy-amenable features of cities with a proven association with pollution levels can be targeted to protect population health and the environment. These characteristics include city density, fragmentation, congestion, quality of public transportation and quantity of green spaces (Gouveia et al., 2021[37]). 
Although air-quality standards have been established in some Latin American countries, these are frequently exceeded. Most cities in the region do not have standards for fuel efficiency, vehicle emissions or fuel quality, which are generally considered to be clean transportation standards. Air quality standards are not consistently enforced in many Caribbean countries.
 Recent trends and developments
In Central and South America, rapid urban development and industrialisation, as well as increased energy transformation and consumption are the main contributors to air pollution. The high population exposure to air pollution in the Caribbean islands reflect the high population density combined with a more concentrated network of road infrastructure. The reverse is true for South America. Socio-economic inequality also plays a significant role in exposure to pollution, the latter being negatively correlated with per capita household income (Libertun de Duren et al., 2022[38]).
Exposure to air pollution is particularly high in big urban areas where economic activity is concentrated and where demand for mobility is highest. Cities worst affected by air pollution include Monterrey, Guadalajara, Mexico City, Cochabamba, Santiago, Lima, Bogotá, Medellín, Montevideo and San Salvador, all of which exceed the concentration levels recommended by the World Health Organisation (WHO) (WHO, 2022[31]). Large differences also exist between population and income groups, with worse outdoor air pollution affecting households located near roads and industrial sites, and worse indoor air pollution due to the use of solid fuels (such as coal, firewood, anthracite and charcoal) as the principal domestic fuel. In some cases, differences in air exposure are linked to inequalities in the development, implementation and compliance of environmental law, policy, and regulation (PAHO, 2023[30]).
Mean population exposure to PM2.5 has slowly decreased from an average of 23 micrograms per cubic metre (µ/m3) across the LAC region in 1990 to 18µ/m3 in 2019, remaining significantly above the guideline set by the WHO of annual mean concentrations of PM2.5 not exceeding 5 µ/m3,6. Currently, all countries in the region exceed this guideline , the highest being Peru, Suriname, Trinidad and Tobago, Belize, Grenada and Chile (Figure 13).
In Latin American cities, private motorised transport is responsible for 75% of CO2 emissions and produces 82% of PM10 pollutants. Vehicle ownership in the LAC region reached 200 vehicles per thousand inhabitants on average in 2015, with the countries with the highest rates being Barbados, Suriname, Bahamas, Argentina and Mexico. In absolute terms, countries with the highest number of combustion vehicles are Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Colombia, Venezuela, Puerto Rico and Peru (OICA, n.d.[36]). 
The higher population density in the Caribbean islands, combined with a more concentrated network of road infrastructure are likely to be behind high population exposure to air pollution, more so than a high motorisation rate. The reverse is true for South America. Socio-economic inequality also plays a significant role in exposure to pollution, the latter being negatively correlated with per capita household income (Libertun de Duren et al., 2022[38]).
Fine particulate emissions are responsible for a large number of premature deaths in LAC countries. Linked to respiratory infections, ischemic heart disease and stroke, cancer and chronic respiratory diseases, an average of 260 premature deaths per million inhabitants is attributed to PM2.5 across the region (Figure 14). This is an increase from an average of 231 premature deaths in 1990. Mortality rates attributable to pollution are however higher in the LAC region than in North America, Europe and Central Asia, and Middle East and North Africa (WHO, 2022[31]). 
In 2019, chronic respiratory diseases caused over 180 000 deaths in the LAC region, most of which were due to chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Brazil represent 43% of them in 2019, followed by Mexico, Colombia, Venezuela, Peru, Cuba, Ecuador and Bolivia. Chronic respiratory diseases account for a significant proportion of premature deaths (PAHO, 2021[39])7 and healthy years lost to disease, measured by Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs); for instance, over 3 million DALYs were lost in South America in 2019, followed by 1.25 million in Central America, and 430 thousand in the Caribbean (PAHO, 2021[39]).
Air pollution carries with it a sizeable cost to society, stemming from mortality from exposure to fine particulates, morbidity, such as labour productivity losses, treatment costs and willingness to pay to avoid illness, as well as costs from impacts other than on human health, such as impacts on built structures, agricultural productivity and ecosystem health. The welfare cost from mortality alone accounts for just under 3% of GDP in Latin America and the Caribbean. While increased by X% since 1990 it has remained stagnant throughout the past three decades (Figure 15).
Countries in the Caribbean in particular face a higher welfare cost, with the highest costs experienced in Barbados, reaching 7% of GDP in 2019, Dominica, Grenada, Dominican Republic, Jamaica, and Antigua and Barbuda. This is due to the compounding of high exposure to the pollutant, fragile healthcare systems leading to a high proportion of premature deaths from said exposure, and the high marginal cost of enhancing safety. Amongst others, the high marginal cost of enhancing safety could be due to health financing systems, the cost of healthcare and the cost sick leave, all influencing the willingness to pay for reducing the risks posed by air pollution. The countries most impacted in South America include Bolivia, with a welfare cost from mortality of 4% of GDP in 2019, Chile, Peru, Argentina, and Colombia, all of which are above the LAC average. The social cost of exposure to PM2.5 is greater still than that of mortality alone.
 Comparability, interpretation and data availability
International data on emissions of PM2.5 are available for many but not all LAC countries. For example, data on mortality attributed to air pollution exposure, and the associated welfare cost is not available for 25 LAC territories, including many Caribbean islands, as well as Uruguay, Venezuela, Suriname and Guatemala. What is more, the estimation methods for emissions, the scope of sources and particles included in estimations differ from one country to another. Moreover, PM2.5 is one of many air pollutants jeopardising health outcomes, such as carbon monoxide, NOx, SOx, lead, ozone and other particulate matter such as PM10. Nevertheless, being the pollutant that poses the greatest health to human health globally, the OECD collects limited data on other pollutants, focusing on population exposure to, and mortality and welfare costs from PM2.5.
Exposure indicators provide only a partial view of air pollution severity and consequences aggregated across the entire population. Importantly, there is generally no “safe level” of exposure for many pollutants. Even where guideline or target exposures are met, substantial public health and economic benefits can be realised through further improvements in air quality. Better estimates are needed for exposure to both outdoor and indoor air pollution. The accuracy of exposure estimates varies considerably by location. Accuracy is poorer in areas with few monitoring stations and in areas with very high concentrations.  Particular attention should be paid to exposure of sensitive groups and quantitative impact on human health (and associated distributional and equity issues). Although many important gaps remain, available data are improving.
Welfare costs only account for mortality, while impacts stemming from air pollution and their associated costs are more widely spread, including morbidity, and costs associated to non-human health impacts.
