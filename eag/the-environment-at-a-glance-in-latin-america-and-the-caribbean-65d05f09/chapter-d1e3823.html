Spotlight on climate change

	  Emissions of greenhouse gases (GHGs) from human activities disturb the radiative energy balance of the earth-atmosphere system. They exacerbate the natural greenhouse effect, leading to temperature changes and other disruption of the earth's climate. Land use changes and forestry also play a role by altering the amount of greenhouse gases captured or released by carbon sinks

Climate change adaptation - Impacts and risks
 Key messages
 
  • Climate change impacts are significant in the LAC region where economic and social vulnerability are compounded by a close relationship with the environment. 13 of the 50 countries most affected by climate change worldwide are in the region. The number of extreme climate‑related weather events in LAC increased (+62%) in most countries between 2001 and 2022.
  • Population exposure to heat stress has increased by + 0.6°C in 2021 compared to the 1981-2010 average. Temperature increases were higher in Central America (+0.85°C). In 2021, 9% of the population in LAC countries was exposed to more than 2 weeks of hot summer days, a 3% increase compared to 1979. The most affected country is Paraguay with nearly 90% of the population exposed to more than 2 weeks of hot summer days, compared to only 28% of in 1979.
  • In 2020, LAC countries had 6.9% of their cropland exposed to more than one week of extreme precipitation, an increase from 5.2% since 2000. In some countries such as Suriname, Guyana, and Trinidad and Tobago, this share is much higher and reached 64%, 59%, and 29% respectively. These extreme precipitations trigger the destruction of crops, landslides, or other extreme events.
  • Across the LAC countries soil moisture on croplands, which monitor the intensity of droughts, decreased by around 2.5%, on average, between the 1981-2010 and 2017-2021. Soil moisture anomaly in cropland affect primarily South America, although almost all LAC countries have experienced a decline.
  • Forest areas potentially exposed to wildfires is very large (32%), presenting an imminent risk to populations and ecosystems. In some countries such as Jamaica, Paraguay, Mexico and El Salvador over 70% of the forest area is exposed to wildfires.
  • On average, 5% of the LAC population and 4% of buildings are affected by river flooding. South America is the most affected sub-region with about 12% of the population and 10% of building exposed.
  • As a whole, the region achieved the 2020 Aichi target to protect at least 17% of terrestrial and inland water, and 10% of coastal and marine areas. In 2022, the LAC region has designated 24% of total land area and 21% of the Exclusive Economic Zones, as terrestrial and marine protected areas, respectively. Progress remain to be done to achieve the newly adopted CBD Global Biodiversity Framework target to conserve and manage 30% of national territory including terrestrial, inland water, and coastal and marine areas by 2030.
 Context
Climate change generates significant impacts on the environment, the economy and human welfare. It threatens ecosystems and biodiversity, affects water resources, human settlements and the frequency and scale of extreme weather events, with significant effects on food security, human well-being, socio-economic activities and output. These impacts can generate considerable losses from both extreme events (such as hurricanes) and slow onset events (such as the sea level rise) and exacerbate socio-economic inequalities.
Some impacts will gradually affect economic processes, such as those associated with rising temperatures, while others may be more acute and periodic such as the increased intensity and frequency of climate-related hazards. Climate-related hazards put populations and economic assets at risk through more frequent and severe hot temperature extremes, marine heatwaves, heavy precipitation, droughts, intense tropical cyclones and reductions in Arctic Sea ice, snow cover and permafrost. Additional threats include wildfires, coastal floods, and sea-level rises (IPCC, 2021[32]). 
Climate change is closely interconnected with biodiversity. Environmental changes driven by climate change are disturbing natural habitats and species in many ways, adding to the threats to biodiversity from human activities. This in turn intensifies the effects of climate change, which cause biodiversity loss. The loss of biological diversity reduces ecosystem resilience and increases vulnerability to climate-related threats. Pressures on biodiversity [from human activities] can be physical (e.g. habitat alteration and fragmentation through changes in land use and land cover, over-exploitation of natural resources), chemical (toxic contamination, acidification, oil spills, other pollution from human activities) or biological (e.g. alteration of population dynamics and species structure through invasive alien species or the commercial use of wildlife resources). 
 Policy challenges
The main challenge is to adapt and manage current and future risks from climate change, protect vulnerable populations and enable the restoration of environmental and other assets that have been lost or degraded. This implies:
  • Integrating adaptation concerns into economic and sectoral policies, and effectively integrating climate and biodiversity policies.
  • Encourage investments in products and natural assets that help reduce the impact of climate change and build resilient infrastructures.
  • Reforming and removing environmentally harmful subsidies and strengthening the role of biodiversity-relevant taxes, fees and charges, as well as other economic instruments such as payments for ecosystem services, biodiversity offsets and tradable permits (such as transferable quotas for fisheries).
  • Implementing nature-based solutions (NbS) that can achieve synergies across NDCs and the GBF’s National Biodiversity Strategies and Action Plans (NBSAPs) to adapt to or reduce climate change risks while ensuring human well-being and biodiversity benefits.
  • Helping coastal communities adapt to the risks stemming from rising sea levels, extreme weather events and flooding. 
 Measuring progress and performance
Progress and performance can be assessed against domestic objectives and international goals and commitments on climate change and biodiversity.
  • Climate change adaptation is part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development (New York, September 2015) under Goal 13. Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts with targets related to the resilience and adaptive capacity to climate-related hazards and natural disasters in all countries and to education, awareness-raising and human and institutional capacity on climate change adaptation, impact reduction and early warning.
Moreover, the UNFCCC has established specific commitments associated with adaptation plans and at COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh (6-18 November 2022) Parties decided to establish a Loss and Damage fund for countries vulnerable to droughts, floods, rising sea level and other disasters attributed to climate change, which is particularly relevant for the Caribbean islands. At the time of writing, no decision has been reached on the sources or recipients of such funding.
  • Biodiversity is an integral part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development (New York, September 2015) under Goal 15 “Protect, restore and promote sustainable use of terrestrial ecosystems, sustainably manage forests, combat desertification, and halt and reverse land degradation and halt biodiversity loss”, and under Goal 14 “Conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas and marine resources for sustainable development”.
The main international agreement on biodiversity protection is the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD). It entered into force on 29 December 1993 with three main objectives: (1) The conservation of biological diversity; (2) The sustainable use of the components of biological diversity; (3) The fair and equitable sharing of the benefits arising out of the utilization of genetic resources. At its 15th Conference of the Parties (COP15) in Montreal from 7-19 December 2022, where the Post-2020 Biodiversity Framework was adopted, comprising 21 targets and 10 milestones for 2030. Targets include the designation of at least 30% of Earth’s land and sea areas as protected for conservation; the reduction of nutrients lost to the environment by at least half, pesticides by at least two thirds; the elimination of discharges of plastic waste; and the reduction of harmful incentives for biodiversity by at least 50 billion USD per year.
The indicators presented in this section describe selected climate hazards and related exposure levels, and their inter-connection with biodiversity. They build on data available for most LAC countries. These data derive from work on climate hazards carried out under the International Programme for Action on Climate (IPAC) (see below) and from work on protected areas drawing on the WDPA (UN-WCMC).

 Exposure to climate hazards
 Measurement framework
To track the most significant impacts of climate change and inform adaptation policies, the OECD has developed a set of indicators to monitor climate-related hazards and exposure to these hazards (Maes et al., 2022[33]). The indicator set is based on the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change’s (IPCC) conceptualisation of climate risk, which considers climate-related hazard, exposure, and vulnerability as the key dimensions (Figure 15).
A climate-related hazard is a “potentially damaging climate-related physical event, phenomenon, or human activity that may cause the loss of life or injury, property damage, social and economic disruption or environmental degradation” (UNDRRR, 2020[35]). Climate-related hazards can include both sudden onset hazards (i.e., event-driven hazards such as heatwaves or cyclones) and slow onset hazards (i.e., long-term changes in the mean and variability of climate patterns such as mean precipitation or temperature).
The indicators that can support LAC countries to understand the impact of climate-related hazards and the exposure of people and assets to these hazards, focus on seven types: (1) extreme temperature, (2) extreme precipitation, (3) drought, (4) wildfire, (5) wind threats, (6) river flooding and (7) coastal flooding. These are key natural hazards, influenced by climate change, with important impacts on people and assets (Maes et al., 2022[33]).
 Recent trends and developments
Climate change impacts are especially acute in LAC where economic and social vulnerability are compounded by a very close social and economic relationship with the environment. Warming temperatures, extreme precipitation events leading to floods, landslides and droughts, sea level rise, coastal erosion, ocean and lake acidification resulting in coral bleaching, and storm surges are expected to increase in frequency and severity, with adverse socio‑economic consequences on the region (OECD et al., 2022[5]). Thirteen of fifty countries most affected by climate change worldwide are in the region (German Watch, 2021[36]).
Around 18% of the 11 072 climate‑related extreme weather events registered worldwide between 1970 and 2019 occurred in LAC (WMO, 2021[37]). An average of 17 hurricanes per year and 23 category‑5 hurricanes were recorded between 2000 and 2019, mostly affecting Caribbean and Central American countries (OCHA, 2020[38]). 
The average number of extreme climate‑related weather events in LAC increased (+62%) in most countries from 0.55 annual events during the period 1980-2000 to 0.89 during the period 2001-2022 (EM-DAT, n.d.[39]).1 Figure 16 presents the average number of climate-related hazards identified in the LAC region between 2016 and 2020 (Maes et al., 2022[33]). It shows the areas most affected by critical or extreme events, or climate-related hazard “hotspots”, helping to identify areas where countries need to focus on and consider developing integrated adaptation, recovery and emergency actions, since they represent.

Temperature Change
Over the past decades, population exposure to heat stress has increased significantly. In 2021, LAC countries have experienced an average increase in surface temperature of around 0.6°C compared to the 1981-2010 average. Temperature increases were higher in Central America (0.85°C on average) and more modest in the Caribbeans (0.5°C). Guatemala and Belize experienced the highest increases in temperature (over 1°C) (Figure 17). 
In 2021, on average, 9% of the population in LAC countries was exposed to more than 2 weeks of hot summer days (measured as a daily maximum temperature above 35 °C), a 3% increase since 1979. This average, however, masks important national variations. For example, nearly 90% of Paraguay’s population is exposed to more than 2 weeks of hot summer days, compared to only 28% in 1979 (Figure 18). 
For tropical countries, high temperatures are best represented by the prevalence of tropical nights. In 2021, 77% of the population in Caribbean countries was exposed to at least 2 weeks of tropical nights (measured as a minimum temperature above 20°C) (Figure 19).

Extreme Precipitation
Countries dependent on agricultural production such as in the LAC region, are more vulnerable to increasing temperatures combined with extreme rainfall events. Many LAC countries are experiencing an increase in extreme precipitation, which can trigger the destruction of crops, landslides, or other extreme events. In 2020, LAC countries had 6.9% of their cropland exposed to more than one week of extreme precipitation (when precipitation exceeds the 99th percentile of the reference period 1981-2010), an increase from 5.2% since 2000. However, in some countries such as Suriname, Guyana, and Trinidad and Tobago, this share is much higher and reached 64%, 59%, and 29% respectively. Moreover, they have experienced a significant increase since 2000, in particular in Surinam, Guyana, Nicaragua and Guatemala (Figure 20).

Droughts
For many years South America has been affected by severe levels of drought and poor water availability. In Central America freshwater resources per capita is projected to decrease by at least 82% in 2100, compared to 2005, while total rainfall is expected to decrease by 11% in 2050 (ECLAC, 2010[40]). These trends are expected to decrease agricultural productivity generating concerns over food security (IPCC, 2022[41]). 
Across the LAC countries cropland soil moisture, which monitor the intensity of droughts, decreased, on average by 2.5% between the 1981-2010 and 2017-2021. Cropland soil moisture anomaly affected primarily South America, although almost all LAC countries have experienced a decline. The countries most affected by droughts are Paraguay, Argentina, and Brazil, which experienced a decline in cropland soil moisture of more than 8.6%, 7.7%, and 5.8%, respectively (Figure 21).

Wildfires
Wildfires are an increasing concern for the countries of the LAC region. For example, in the summer of 2023 (January-February), Chile experienced wildfires that have generated a significant loss of life and infrastructure. 
The forested area potentially exposed to wildfires is very large (32% on average) in the LAC region, presenting an imminent risk to populations and ecosystems. In some countries such as Jamaica, Paraguay, Mexico and El Salvador, over 70% of the forested area is exposed to wildfires. The proportions are smaller in Brazil and Venezuela (46% and 40% respectively), but nevertheless represent large areas due to the size of these countries (over 2.2 million km2), an area larger than the size of Mexico (Figure 22).

Wind threats
Climate change also exacerbates storms, which not only lead to the loss of human life, but can destroy economic infrastructure, increasing the costs of loss and damage, as well as future replacement and construction. Tropical cyclones are limited to a subset of LAC countries due to their geographic position. In the Caribbean, almost all countries are exposed to tropical cyclones. Belize and Mexico also are heavily affected, with more than 50% of their populations and built-up areas exposed to extreme tropical cyclones (Figure 23).2

River flooding
Due to the type of ecosystem and the location of most populations, river flooding is not a major concern in the LAC region. On average, 5% of the LAC population and 4% of buildings are affected by river flooding. South America is the most affected sub-region with about 12% of the population and 10% of building exposed. The countries most affected are Suriname, Guyana and Argentina (Figure 24 and Figure 25).

Box 3 The increasing cost of climate-related hazards in LAC
One study shows global GDP could be reduced by as much as 4% in 2050 due to climate change and that lower-income and lower‑middle income countries are more likely face economic losses (S&P Global, 2022[42]). The costs from natural disasters alone are estimated at USD 280 billion in 2021, representing approximately 0.29% of global GDP (Munich RE, 2022[43]).
In the Dominican Republic, the heavy rains of 2016 generated severe economic losses in crops such as plantain, cassava and sweet potato. During the hurricane season of 2017, an estimated loss of USD 52 million was recorded due to lower touristic activity. In the case of Haiti and Puerto Rico, two of the three most affected countries in the world between 1999 and 2018, annual GDP losses reached 2.38% and 3.76%, respectively (OECD et al., 2022[5]).
Similar economic costs can be caused by the concentration of multiple non‑extreme events in a short period, especially when there are conditions of high exposure and vulnerability in the country. Many countries in LAC are experiencing a fall in agricultural productivity and tourism, as well as climate‑driven migration and high reconstruction costs after natural disasters. 
Natural disasters increase expenditures and thus have direct impacts on fiscal deficits and public revenues, affecting the long-term fiscal sustainability of governments. On average, a natural disaster results in costs of 0.8% and 1.1% of GDP, for lower middle‑income and low‑income countries respectively (OECD et al., 2022[5]). For many LAC countries the fiscal capacity to deal with the increasing impacts of climate change is limited. On average, a natural disaster results in an increase in the fiscal deficit of 0.3% of GDP for upper‑middle‑income countries, 0.8% for lower‑middle‑income countries and 0.9% for low‑income countries (OECD et al., 2022[5]). 
For all the costs of extreme events, the costs of inaction may be even higher. In the case of the Caribbean, the annual cost of inaction to deal with the effects of sea level rise is estimated to reach USD 22 billion per year by 2050 (10% of GDP) and USD 46 billion by 2100 (22% of GDP) (OECD et al., 2022[5]). If environmental degradation is not addressed soon, LAC governments will have to add the costs of climate change to those of social vulnerability that could have unprecedented economic and social consequences.
 Comparability, interpretation and data availability
Natural climate variability and human-caused climate change increase risks attributed to climate-related hazards, influencing the frequency, intensity, extent and duration of extreme weather and climate events. Climate-related hazard, exposure and vulnerability are key dimensions of disaster risk. Measuring all dimensions that encompass risk is difficult because of data limitations, particularly on a global scale. For example, there are no commonly used data sources for assessing vulnerability globally.
The indicators presented are a selection of a broader set of indicators on climate-related hazards developed by Maes et al. (2022) they are based on data from a number of official sources such as the Copernicus Climate Data Store (CDS), the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) and the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA), among others. For a detailed discussion see Maes et al. (2022). All regional averages presented here are simple, non-weighted averages over the countries in the LAC region. They therefore do not consider differences in area or population and represent the exposure of an average LAC country to natural hazards, but not the actual share of land or population of the region that is exposed.
 Climate impacts and biodiversity
Environmental changes, driven by climate change and climate-related hazards, are disturbing natural habitats and species in many ways, adding to threats on biodiversity from human activities. Moreover, given ecosystems natural capacity for carbon sequestration the protection and sustainable management of biodiversity is a key strategy for climate mitigation.
Biodiversity and ecosystem services are integral elements of the natural capital of Latin America and the Caribbean, and the loss of biodiversity is a major concern (IPBES, 2018[44]). Many economic activities rely on the quality of the region’s ecosystems, such as the tourism industry, agriculture, fisheries and forestry.
LAC’s rich natural environment is an opportunity for economic development but also generates considerable challenges, risks, and vulnerabilities. Land use change, deforestation, increased nutrient pollution from intensive agricultural practices, and mining production are some of the main pressures on the environment caused by the exploitation of these natural resources. Retreating glaciers, bleaching coral reefs and loss of ecosystem services undermine the ability of ecosystems to provide a shield against growing climate‑related risks and creates additional vulnerabilities. Biodiversity loss reduces ecosystem resilience and increases vulnerability to threats such as the negative impacts of climate change. The LAC region is home to major ecosystems that are directly under pressure because of climate change and other environmental impacts. The loss of biodiversity is a key concern in Latin America and the Caribbean, declining at twice the rate observed across OECD countries(WWF, 2020[43]). While Chile, Ecuador and Mexico have experienced the largest declines, every country in the region is considered to have “high‑risk” rates of decline, based on the United Nation’s Red List Index (UN DESA, n.d.[44]). 
 Recent trends and developments
Protected areas are legally designated areas for the protection and maintenance of biological diversity and of natural resources and the associated cultural resources, managed through administrative or other effective means. Although national classifications differ, the International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUCN) has developed an internationally agreed definition of protected areas, which classify them as strict nature reserves and wilderness areas (I), national parks (II), natural monuments or features (III), habitat or species management areas (IV), protected landscapes or seascapes (V), and protected areas with sustainable use of natural resources (VI).
In 2022, the region has designated just under 5 million km2 of terrestrial protected areas and over 4 million km2 of marine protected areas, representing 24% of total land area of countries of the region, and 21% of the Exclusive Economic Zones, respectively (Figure 26). As a whole, the region achieved the 2020 Aichi target to protect at least 17% of terrestrial and inland water, and 10% of coastal and marine areas. Progress remain to be done to achieve the newly adopted CBD Global Biodiversity Framework target to conserve and manage 30% of national territory including terrestrial, inland water, and coastal and marine areas by 2030. 
The share of terrestrial protected areas has increased steadily over the past few decades from an average of under 5% in 1980. Countries driving the trend include Belize, Bolivia, Brazil, Guatemala, Panama, Trinidad and Tobago and Venezuela, all of which have already surpassed the Aichi 2030 target. Nevertheless, most of these countries rely on the least stringent3 designation of protected areas (such as protected landscapes) rather than strict nature reserves, national parks and wilderness areas (IUCN categories I-II).
Marine protected areas are less commonly designated throughout the LAC region, with 5 countries reaching the CBD Global Biodiversity Framework 2030 target. The share of marine protected areas across the region represented about 21% of the exclusive economic zone (EEZ) in 2022. It has increased slowly from 1960, remaining below 5% until 2015. Only 10 territories designated strict nature reserves, national parks or wilderness areas (IUCN categories I-II).
Nature-based solutions (NbS) generally refer to a broad set of actions that protect, sustainably manage, restore, or modify ecosystems to address policy objectives such as climate change mitigation or adaptation while simultaneously providing biodiversity benefits. These have become increasingly important in climate action and countries are including NbS in their NDCs as part of their overall policy efforts. Twelve LAC countries explicitly mention NbS in their NDCs. For example, Paraguay’s NDC proposes to “protect and restore wetlands and water sources”; Argentina is committed to “strengthen and expand national Protected Areas”, Costa Rica is creating a methodological guide for NbS in infrastructure, and; Chile is planting 200,000 hectares of forest, a third of which are native species(EUROCLIMA, 2022[45]).

 Comparability, interpretation and data availability
Data on protected areas is available for most LAC countries. The definitions, although harmonised by the World Conservation Monitoring Centre, may vary across countries. The extent, and management types, of terrestrial and marine protected areas can indicate countries’ efforts to safeguard habitats and species as well as landscapes shaped by human-environment interactions that are valued for cultural or other reasons. Protected areas are not always representative of national biodiversity or sufficiently connected. Indicators on the extent of protected areas do not reflect the effectiveness of the management of these areas.
