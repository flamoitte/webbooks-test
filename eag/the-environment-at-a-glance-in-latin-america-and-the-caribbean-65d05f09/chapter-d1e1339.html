Climate change mitigation
 Key messages
 
  • Under the Paris Agreement, 33 LAC countries have submitted GHG emission reduction commitments to the UNFCCC through their NDCs, to be achieved by 2030. However, only 20 countries presented NDCs that are clear enough to infer targets for 2030 and beyond. Only 16 countries have committed to net zero targets by 2050 or earlier1 and only six countries cover all GHGs in their pledges.
  • The LAC region contributed about 6.7% of global greenhouse gas emissions in 2019. 2 Emissions from agriculture and from land use change and forestry (LUCF) play an important role, followed by transport.
  • GHG emissions per capita in LAC remain significantly lower than the OECD average, partly reflecting lower consumption levels. 
  • The LAC region achieved a relative decoupling of GHG emissions from economic growth since 2014. However, oerall GHG emissions (excluding LULUCF) have grown by approximately 61% from 1990 to 2019, particularly in the energy sector driven by increased emissions from the transport sector as well as electricity and heat production. 
  • Energy supply in the LAC region is dominated by fossil fuels (69% in 2020), although renewables, mainly hydropower and biofuels, account for a much larger share (30%) than in the OECD (12%) and the rest of the world (15%). More than two-thirds (69%) of electricity is produced from renewables, an increase by 9 percentage points since 2015, 78% of which is composed of hydroelectricity. 
  • Mean population exposure to PM2.5 has slowly decreased since 1990 across the LAC region, but at 18µg/m3 in 2019, it remains significantly above the WHO guideline of not exceeding an 5 µg/m3 annually. Linked to respiratory infections, an average of 260 premature deaths per million inhabitants is attributed to population exposure to fine particulate matter (PM2.5).
 Context
Greenhouse gas emissions from human activities disturb the radiative energy balance of the earth-atmosphere system. They exacerbate the natural greenhouse effect, leading to temperature changes and other disruption of the earth's climate. Changes in land use and land cover also play a role by altering the GHGs captured or released by carbon sinks. Carbon dioxide (CO2) from the combustion of fossil fuels and changes in land-use are the principal contributors to greenhouse gases and a key factor in countries’ ability to mitigate climate change.
GHGs are often released from the same sources as air pollutants such as fine particulate matter (PM2.5). Hence mitigation efforts can have co-benefits by improving air quality, and vice-versa, with consequent benefits on human health, especially in densely populated areas. 
 Policy challenges
The main challenge is to mitigate GHG emissions, in an economically efficient and socially acceptable manner. This implies a range of low-carbon strategies to decouple GHG emissions from economic growth and which may involve different specific policies depending on local conditions such as increasing the share of renewable energy sources in the supply mix, reducing energy intensity by adopting energy-efficient production processes, increasing the energy efficiency of consumer goods and services, and protecting and expanding carbon sinks such as forest areas and wetlands. 
Domestic mitigation efforts must be aligned across other policy areas. Recognising the connection between GHG emissions and local air pollution, suggests developing strategies that reduce emissions of greenhouse gases and air pollutants at minimum cost, thus implementing “win-win” solutions for both climate and health policy objectives. Effective pollution prevention and control measures are especially important, these need to be tailored to local circumstances as both the sources of air pollution and severity of exposure vary across and within countries. 
Measures should be prioritised in terms of their health benefit, for example more stringent measures are required in densely populated areas or for emission sources located upwind from urban areas. Other relevant policies include mainstreaming sustainable construction and transport in urban and rural planning and moving from linear to circular economy business models. Finally, behavioural and lifestyle changes may ultimately be the key for long-term mitigation objectives.
Ensuring a proper mix of policy instruments -such as carbon pricing, investing in new technologies and removing government subsidies and other support for fossil fuels- plays an important role in supporting mitigation efforts (Climate policy instruments). It also implies considering agriculture and land-use in national mitigation policies and strengthening the capacity of the environment to store carbon by protecting ecosystems, an especially important challenge in the LAC region. Moreover, given that climate change is a global phenomenon, mitigation efforts must be placed in the context of carbon flows associated with international trade. 
 Measuring progress and performance 
Environmental performance can be assessed against domestic objectives and international goals and commitments to reduce GHG and air pollutant emissions.
The main international agreement is the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (Box 1). LAC countries have established their climate mitigation objectives to 2030 through their Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) (Table 2). Several countries have gone further, committing to carbon neutrality. Climate change mitigation is also part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development  under Goal 13 Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts with targets related to the integration of climate change measures into national policies, strategies and planning, as well as increase education, awareness-raising and human and institutional capacity on climate change mitigation.
Reducing negative impacts of degraded air quality is part of the 2030 Agenda under Goal 3 “Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages” and under Goal 11 “Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable”. 
 
Box 1 The UNFCCC and the Paris Agreement
The centrepiece of the international climate governance regime is the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) negotiated at the United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED) in Rio de Janeiro in 1992. The Convention established a broad set of substantive principles, including the “common but differentiated responsibilities”, but does not contain binding commitments to reduce GHG emissions. 
At the 21st session of the Conference of Parties (COP) in Paris, on 12 December 2015, Parties to the UNFCCC reached a landmark agreement to combat climate change and to accelerate and intensify the actions and investments needed for a sustainable low-carbon future. The Paris Agreement (PA) sets out the goal of limiting average global warming to 2ºC above pre-industrial levels and identifies a further need for efforts to confine the increase to 1.5ºC. 
The basis of the PA is a non-binding “bottom-up” approach. Countries submit contributions reflecting their highest possible ambition; these are referred to as Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). NDCs present targets to progressively mitigate GHG emissions and enhance their adaptive capacity to climate change, together with information necessary for clarity, transparency and understanding.
The Paris Agreement has been instrumental in increasing climate mitigation ambitions. The implementation of these commitments has led to considerably lower global GHG emissions than previously projected. New estimates considering the PA commitments suggest that global GHG emissions will peak before 2030 (UNFCCC, 2022). Further, in COP26, the Glasgow Climate Pact made a substantial contribution to strengthening countries’ implementation capacity by completing the Paris Agreement’s rulebook on market mechanisms and non-market approaches, as well as the requirements for the transparent reporting of climate actions.
However, this is still not enough, and estimates suggest that current targets will not achieve the goals set out by the PA (UNFCCC, 2022). Therefore, facing the climate emergency depends on countries both substantially increasing their ambition as well as ensuring the implementation of those targets, through effective climate action

  • GHG emission trends 
  • Share of GHG emissions by region and LAC sub-region
  • GHG emissions by sector 
  • CO2 productivity and CO2 intensity per capita

 Climate objectives in the LAC region
Under the Paris Agreement, 33 LAC countries have submitted GHG emission reduction commitments to the UNFCCC through their NDCs, to be achieved by 2030. However, there are differences across countries in terms of clarity, information details, gas and sectoral coverage of the targets set. Only 25 countries presented economy-wide NDCs (accounting for approximately 92.5% of regional emissions in 2019, including LULUCF). GHG emissions data for the LAC region is limited as most LAC countries don’t produce up-to-date official and comprehensive GHG emission inventories.
By 2022, 29 Latin American countries had updated their NDC targets, since their first submission,3 many of which presented an increase in ambition. Moreover, 16 have committed to net zero targets by 2050 or earlier.4 However countries have made commitments with different legal status. Chile and Colombia are the only countries whose targets are enshrined in a climate law, while Jamaica, Paraguay, and Trinidad and Tobago have declared a net zero pledge. Eleven countries (Antigua and Barbuda, Argentina, Barbados, Belize, Brazil, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Ecuador, Grenada, Peru and Uruguay) have included their target in a policy or planning document, including Nationally Determined Contributions and Long-Term Strategies submitted to the UNFCCC.
The scope of gases covered in net zero targets varies across countries. Chile, Colombia and Costa Rica provide detailed reduction and removal targets by sector, with separate emission reduction and removal targets, and with transparent pathways for the LULUCF sector. This is not the case for other LAC countries, most of which do not include information on the target’s emission coverage or sectoral breakdown, and do not have separate reduction and removal targets, transparent assumptions on removals, or information on the anticipated pathway towards net zero. Only six countries cover all GHGs in their pledges (Argentina, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ecuador and Peru), leaving a majority of countries with either unspecified pledges, or in the case of Uruguay, pledges limited to CO2. The latter is of concern in a region where emissions from methane-intensive sectors such as agriculture contribute to over a quarter of total emissions, missing the opportunity to meaningfully achieve net zero. 
Table 2 presents the targets and commitments presented by LAC countries. Note, that some countries have targets that imply an increase in current GHG emissions (e.g. the Bahamas, the Dominican Republic, Honduras, Haiti, Saint Lucia, Mexico, and Peru) equivalent to an increase in emissions of 286.3 MTCO2e, or a 7.1% increase compared to total GHG emissions in LAC in 2019 (including LULUCF). However, more than 10 LAC countries do not have a clear target covering all sectors and all gases. Therefore, overall, GHG emissions for the region are expected to increase further.
Countries have set out a range of policy approaches to comply with their commitments. These vary from direct regulations, carbon pricing, reducing deforestation, among other approaches and efforts (Climate policy instruments).

 Greenhouse gas emissions
 Recent trends and developments 
Total greenhouse gas emissions trends
Available estimates suggest that gross emissions (excluding Land Use Change and Forestry, or LUCF) rose by 61% between 1990 and 2019 reaching about 3.2 Gt of CO2e in 2019 (Climate Watch, 2022[20]). This represented approximately 6.7% of global GHG emissions, about the same share as in 1990Figure 4. Despite not being a major contributor to climate change, the LAC region has a role to play in global mitigation efforts due to its potential for natural carbon capture, particularly around the Amazon river basin.
Emissions grew steadily in line with GDP until 2014, then stabilised and even decreased, suggesting a relative decoupling. There are significant differences between sub-regions, reflecting their size and the level of economic activity. South America generates most of LAC GHG emissions (71%), followed by Central America (24%) and the Caribbean (4%) (Figure 4). Brazil contributed about 32% of LAC’s gross total GHG emissions in 2019 while the top five emitters (Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Venezuela, and Colombia) contributed about 78% and the top ten emitters contributed 90%5. The overall trend is driven by South America, where emissions increased by 61% between 1990 and 2019, followed by Central America (68%) and the Caribbean (26%) (Figure 5).6

The profile of emissions in LAC differs from that of the EU and OECD countries. In LAC fuel combustion is responsible for most emissions (44.3% of the region’s net emissions, i.e. including LULUCF), followed by agriculture (25.8%) (Climate Watch, 2022[20]). In the OECD area, fuel combustion accounts for 83.6% of net emissions and agriculture only 8.5%. Land use, land use change and forestry (LULUCF) act as a net sink in the OECD, whereas they generate about 20% of net emissions in LAC (Climate Watch, 2022[20]) (Figure 7). 
These trends are driven principally by the emission profile of South America where agriculture and LULUCF account for a third and a fourth of the sub-region’s net emissions, respectively. This reflects the importance of agriculture in the economy as well as the massive deforestation occurring in the region. Forest loss is a major concern, mainly driven by extensive conversion of land for agriculture, forestry, and, to a lesser extent, the expansion of urban areas. Deforestation in Brazil has accelerated since 2012. Nicaragua and Paraguay also experienced high loss rates in the last 20 years, although the areas involved are smaller. On the other hand, Costa Rica and Chile managed to increase their forest cover with successful afforestation policies (OECD et al., 2022[5]). 
Central America has a profile closer to OECD countries, energy generation being the first source of emissions, followed by transport. In the Caribbean, energy generation is also the main source of GHGs but due to the relatively small size of Caribbean islands, transport is only the fourth source of emissions after agriculture and LULUCF (Figure 7).
Since 1990, emissions from almost all sectors have grown continuously, particularly emissions from fuel combustion. This trend was driven by the growth of emissions from transport, followed by electricity and heat production. Emissions from agriculture increased by about 32% due to the rise in livestock breeding in South and Central America until 2010. While emissions from industrial processes and waste are lower, they have significantly increased (193% and 108%, respectively since 1990) (OECD et al., 2022[5]).  
Transport is a major source of GHG emissions, mainly due to the large size of many LAC countries and the reliance on fossil fuels. The Caribbean has one of the highest vehicle ownership rates in the world, with an average of 273 vehicles per 1 000 inhabitants in 2015, as compared to the average across South America of 186, and for Central America of 136 (OICA, n.d.[22]). In LAC countries, the urban layout and the poor quality and availability of public transport favour the use of private vehicles.

CO2 emission intensity
CO2 emissions from fuel combustion increased by 89% between 1990 and 2018 in the region, with some countries increasing their emissions by 317% (Dominican Republic) and others reducing them by 57% (Venezuela) (IEA, 2021[21]). In 2020 emissions dropped by -12% from the previous year due to the COVID-19 pandemic and associated restrictions in activities and mobilities. 
In 2020, the per capita level of production-based CO2 in most LAC countries was significantly lower than the OECD average of 7.5 tonnes per capita, reflecting lower consumption levels. There are wide disparities among countries, per capita CO2 emission ranging from 0.3 tonnes of CO2 in Haiti to 9.8 tonnes of CO2 in Trinidada and Tobago. On the other hand, most LAC countries generate more emissions per unit of GDP, than the OECD average, reflecting less energy efficient processes but also more reliance on energy intensive production processes to sustain the economic activity (Figure 8 and Figure 9). In the region, Costa Rica, Paraguay and Cuba generate less emissions per unit of GDP, while Trinidad and Tobago, Suriname and Venezuela have the lowest productivity.

 Comparability, interpretation and data availability
GHG emissions
Official GHG emission (inventory) data are not regularly compiled and updated in LAC countries. Data and times series available through the UNFCCC and OECD show many gaps, including large emitters. Between 1990 and 2017, official emissions data is missing for at least seven LAC countries in any given year. Missing information is a major impediment to assess trends and progress in mitigation efforts. 
The Climate Watch data, compiled by the World Resource Institute (WRI), cover all parties to the UNFCCC, all gases and the main IPCC sectors. The methodology used follows IPCC’s guidelines to compile sectoral emissions drawing on sources that have been widely adopted including in the IEA’s GHG Emissions from Fossil Fuel Combustion, the FAO’s agriculture and land use emission data, US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)’s non-CO2 gases emission data, and fossil related CO2 emissions from the Global Carbon Project. Differences with existing official data are not explicitly provided in the documentation and are difficult to explain. The data remains the most comprehensive global data source providing general estimates of keys sectors and trends, however, for individual countries, particularly small countries it should be taken with care  (Climate Watch, 2022[20]).
CO2 emissions from fuel combustion
The CO2 emission estimates are affected by the quality of the underlying energy data, but in general the comparability across countries is quite good. Carbon productivity indicators inform on the relative decoupling between economic activity and GHG emissions. They provide insight into how much carbon productivity has improved. Reductions in national emissions can also be achieved by offshoring domestic production and, thus, the related emissions. Evidence of decoupling based on domestic emissions, therefore, may reveal only part of the story.
 Greenhouse gases and air pollution
Climate mitigation and local air pollution policies have multiple co-benefits and synergies. They are especially important in LAC where air pollution is the leading environmental health risk, particularly in vulnerable groups such as the very young and the very old, and a major cause of environmental degradation (PAHO, 2023[23]). 
Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) is a local air pollutant that seriously affects human health, predominantly caused by motorised vehicles, the use of wood as fuel, and wildfires. Chronic exposure, even to moderate levels of PM2.5, substantially increases the risk of heart disease and stroke and is an important cause of premature death in LAC countries (Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, 2019[24]). It also increases the risk of respiratory diseases, including lung cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and respiratory infections. 
Black carbon, a major component of PM2.5, is one of the largest contributors to global warming after CO2. It accelerates the melting of snow and ice increasing climate change by reducing the albedo effect, and affects agricultural yields and food security. There is evidence that pollutant emissions generated by the biomass burning season have affected the precipitation in the Amazon basin which may further increase climate-related hazards (Koren et al., 2004[25]). It has also been associated with the decreasing size of Andean glaciers which have fallen to around 25% since 1964 (Rabatel et al., 2013[26]). In turn, this affects the livelihoods of dozens of million Andean inhabitants which depend the glaciers for drinking water as well as agriculture and the generation of hydropower, a major source of energy in the region, producing 8% of total energy supply across LAC in 2020 (IEA, 2023[27]), and 54% of electricity production(IEA, 2022[9]).
Urban planning and policies to green the transport system have the power to significantly reduce air pollution levels. Understanding the relationship between pollution levels and the urban environment is particularly useful for policymakers in the context of limited air quality data, as policy-amenable features of cities with a proven association with pollution levels can be targeted to protect population health and the environment. These characteristics include city density, fragmentation, congestion, quality of public transportation and quantity of green spaces (Gouveia et al., 2021[28]). 
Although air-quality standards have been established in some LAC countries, these are frequently exceeded. Most cities in the region do not have standards for fuel efficiency, vehicle emissions or fuel quality, which are generally considered to be clean transportation standards. Air quality standards are not consistently enforced in many Caribbean countries.
 Recent trends and developments
In Central and South America accelerated urban development and industrialisation, as well as increased energy production and consumption, are the main contributors to air pollution. The high population exposure to PM2.5 in the Caribbean islands reflects the high population density combined with a concentrated network of road infrastructure. Socio-economic inequality also plays a significant role in exposure to pollution, the latter being negatively correlated with per capita household income (Libertun de Duren et al., 2022[29]).
Exposure to air pollution is particularly high in big urban areas where economic activity is concentrated and where demand for mobility is highest. Cities most affected by air pollution include Monterrey, Guadalajara, Mexico City, Cochabamba, Santiago, Lima, Bogotá, Medellín, Montevideo and San Salvador, all of which exceed the concentration levels recommended by the World Health Organisation (WHO) (WHO, 2022[30]). Large differences also exist between income groups, with worse outdoor air pollution affecting households located near roads and industrial sites, and worse indoor air pollution due to the use of solid fuels (such as coal, firewood, anthracite and charcoal) as the principal domestic fuel. In some cases, differences in air exposure are linked to inequalities in the development, implementation and compliance of environmental law, policy, and regulation (PAHO, 2023[23]).
Individual vehicles are responsible for 75% of CO2 emissions and 82% of PM10 emissions (Vasconcellos, 2019[31]). Vehicle ownership in the LAC region reached 200 vehicles per thousand inhabitants on average in 2015. The countries with the highest rates are Barbados, Suriname, Bahamas, Argentina and Mexico. Nevertheless, motorisation remains much lower in LAC than the OECD, with an average of 532 vehicles per thousand inhabitants in the same period, reflecting lower income levels (OICA, n.d.[22]).
Mean population exposure to PM2.5
Mean population exposure to PM2.5 has slowly decreased from an average of 23 micrograms per cubic metre (µg/m3) across the LAC region in 1990, to 18 µg/m3 in 2019. It remains significantly above the WHO guideline of not exceeding an annual mean PM2.5 concentration of 5 µg/m3, which is the lowest range over which adverse health effects can be observed. Currently, all countries in the region exceed this guideline value, the countries most affected are Peru, Suriname, Trinidad and Tobago, Belize, Grenada and Chile (Figure 10).

Mortality attributed to exposure to PM2.5
Local air pollution is a major health risk in the region. Fine particulate matter concentrations is linked to respiratory infections, ischemic heart disease and stroke, cancer and chronic respiratory diseases. In 2019, chronic respiratory diseases caused over 180 000 deaths in the LAC region, most of which were due to chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Brazil represented 43% of chronic respiratory diseases, followed by Mexico, Colombia, Venezuela, Peru, Cuba, Ecuador and Bolivia. An average of 260 premature deaths per million inhabitants is attributed to PM2.5 across the region (Figure 11), an increase from 231 deaths in 1990. Mortality rates attributable to pollution are higher in the LAC region than other regions (WHO, 2022[30]).

Welfare cost of mortality from exposure to PM2.5
The mortality and morbidity from exposure to air pollution generates considerable economic costs. The welfare cost , associated with premature mortality alone accounts for just under 3% of GDP in Latin America and the Caribbean (Figure 12). Factoring the cost of morbidity, from labour productivity losses, medical treatment, losses in agricultural productivity, among others would increase these further.
Countries in the Caribbean are especially affected. Barbados experienced the highest welfare losses, reaching an estimated 7% of GDP in 2019. Dominica, Grenada, Dominican Republic, Jamaica, and Antigua and Barbuda have costs above 3% of GDP. This is due to the compounding effects of high exposure to the pollutant, fragile healthcare systems leading to a high proportion of premature deaths and the high marginal cost of enhancing safety. Amongst others, the high marginal cost of enhancing safety could be due to health financing systems, the cost of healthcare and the cost of sick leave, all influencing the willingness to pay for reducing the risks posed by air pollution. In South America, the countries most impacted include Bolivia, with an estimated welfare cost from mortality of 4% of GDP in 2019, Chile, Peru, Argentina, and Colombia, all of which are above the LAC average.
 Comparability, interpretation and data availability
International data on emissions of PM2.5 are available for many but not all LAC countries. For example, data on mortality attributed to air pollution exposure, and the associated welfare cost is not available for 25 LAC territories, including many Caribbean islands, as well as Uruguay, Venezuela, Suriname and Guatemala. The estimation methods for emissions, the scope of sources and particles included in estimations differ between countries. PM2.5 is the pollutant that poses the greatest health to human health globally, others include carbon monoxide, NOx, SOx, lead, and ozone.
Exposure indicators provide only a partial view of air pollution severity and consequences aggregated across the entire population. Importantly, there is generally no “safe level” of exposure for many pollutants. Even where guideline or target exposures are met, substantial public health and economic benefits can be realised through further improvements in air quality. Better estimates are needed for exposure to both outdoor and indoor air pollution. The accuracy of exposure estimates varies considerably by location. Accuracy is poorer in areas with few monitoring stations and in areas with very high concentrations. Particular attention should be paid to exposure of sensitive groups and quantitative impact on human health (and associated distributional and equity issues). Although many important gaps remain, available data is improving.
Welfare costs only account for mortality, while impacts stemming from air pollution and their associated costs are more wide-spread, including morbidity, and costs associated to non-human health impacts.
 Energy mix
 Recent trends and developments
Total energy supply (TES) in the LAC region is dominated by fossil fuels (69% of TES in 2020), although renewables account for a much larger share (30%) than in OECD (12%) and the global average (15%) (IEA, 2023[27]). The main sources of renewable energy is hydroelectric power and biofuels, such as firewood and bagasse (OECD et al., 2022[5]). In 2020, hydroelectricity accounted for 26% of total renewable energy supply across LAC, playing a larger role in South America (30%) and Central America (13%), and a lesser role in the Caribbean (1%). The use of renewables varies across the region. In some countries, such as Brazil, Costa Rica, Haiti, Honduras, Nicaragua, Paraguay and Uruguay, the share of renewables in the energy mix is over 40% while it is less than 10% in Argentina, Guyana, Mexico and Trinidad and Tobago (Figure 14). There is very little use of nuclear energy in the region. Although energy produced with coal has been decreasing across the region, many LAC countries remain highly dependent, such as Chile, where 18% of total energy supply was generated from coal in 2021, Brazil with 8%, Colombia with 6% and Mexico with 4% (IEA, 2023[27]). 
In 2020, renewable energy accounted for 69% of LAC electricity generation, an increase by 9 percentage points since 2015, of which 78% from hydroelectricity and 22% from solar, wind, biomass and geothermal (IEA, 2022[9]). Central America has shown the greatest increases in the share of renewables, from 65% in 2000 to 77% of electricity generation in 2020. However, significant variations exist across the region. While Brazil generates 84% of its electric power from renewables, Jamaica relies on imported oil derivatives for 87% of its electricity generation (OECD et al., 2022[5]).
Since renewable energy is focused on hydroelectricity, representing 54% of total electricity generation in LAC in 2020 and 26% of renewable energy (IEA, 2022[9]), there is a significant risk that changing rainfall patterns and increasing drought may affect the capacity of energy systems to adapt. In Chile extended droughts have affected considerably hydroelectric generation capacity.
 
Box 2. Renewables in LAC: The RELAC initiative
Renewables in Latin America and the Caribbean (RELAC) is a regional initiative created in 2019 within the framework of the United Nations Climate Action Summit. It has 15 member countries in the LAC region that have voluntarily agreed to promote renewables with: 1) a concrete goal; 2) a monitoring scheme; and 3) an operating structure designed to support countries in the process. The initiative’s general goals are to accelerate the carbon neutrality of electricity systems in LAC; improve the resilience, competitiveness and sustainability of the sector; and create green jobs, improving air quality and minimising harmful health effects for Latin Americans. To do this, RELAC established a climate action platform for LAC countries and international organisations to enable sharing sustainable solutions.
The RELAC initiative aims to reach at least 70% of renewable energy participation in the region’s electricity mix by 2030. Two conditions are needed to achieve these targets. The first is to invest 1.3% of regional GDP for ten years, equivalent to USD 114 per capita (i.e., USD 80 billion in constant 2010 prices), with some countries needing a greater or lesser proportion of GDP depending on local circumstances. The second is to increase renewables technologies (mostly solar and wind) in line with the targets of Sustainable Development Goal 7 (SDG 7) on access to affordable and clean energy. These actions could create 7 million green jobs and reduce GHG emissions by 30% by 2030.

 Comparability and interpretation
Data quality is not homogeneous for all countries. In some countries, data are based on secondary sources, and where incomplete, estimates were made by the IEA. In general, data are likely to be more accurate for production and trade than for international bunkers or stock changes; and statistics for biofuels and waste are less accurate than those for traditional commercial energy data. The supply structure, which may vary considerably across countries is dependent on final demands by industry, transport and the household sector, and is highly influenced by national energy policies and endowments in energy resources.
